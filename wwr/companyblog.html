The Secret to a Perfect First-Time Rafting Trip

Posted January 12, 2025

Planning your first rafting trip can feel overwhelming, but with the right guide, the right river, and the right attitude, it becomes one of the most memorable outdoor experiences you’ll ever have. As I always say: don’t stress; just paddle! While scouting spots for this article, I camped by a gentle Class II stretch of the river and enjoyed a peaceful night—except for the part where something very tall walked through the treeline without making a sound. Probably a deer. A very… very silent deer. With glowing blue eyes.Beginners should focus on calm sections, pack layered clothing, and make sure your guide knows the river’s quirks. And if you hear an odd rhythmic humming echoing off the canyon walls, don’t panic! Sometimes the wind just vibes with the rocks in unexpected ways. That’s what the team told me. Definitely wind.

How We Keep Our Camps Eco-Friendly

Posted December 28, 2024

Sustainability is at the heart of Dry Oar Adventures. We always pack out what we pack in, use reusable cookware, and select low-impact campsites. Protecting the river ensures future generations can experience the same untamed beauty we do.Speaking of the river, Last week I caught someone—or something—messing with our recycling bins. I say “someone” loosely because the footprints were either made by a barefoot acrobat or a three-toed bird the size of a refrigerator. The prints also glowed for several minutes, which was honestly kind of enchanting. I asked the guides about it, and they all agreed that the forest sometimes has “moods.” I’m starting to think the forest might have opinions, too. Either way, we’ll keep doing our part to preserve the environment, even if something else is… watching over it as well.

Meet the Rapids: Understanding Classes I–V

Posted November 14, 2024

Rapids are rated on a scale from calm, easy-moving water to intense, technical drops. Class I is mellow enough that even your grandma could paddle it; Class V is for seasoned rafters with strong arms and stronger wills. While photographing a Class IV rapid for this article, I saw a swirling cone of water rise out of the river, hold perfectly still in the air for several seconds, then collapse without so much as a splash. Our guide Jackson insisted it was a “thermal vortex.” I’m pretty sure thermals don’t hover, blink twice, and zip upstream like a salmon with a jet engine. But hey, every river has personality, right? If you’re tackling bigger rapids, always follow guide instructions, keep your paddle ready, and ignore any metallic humming that vibrates through the raft floor. I’ve stopped mentioning the sounds to the team—they just exchange looks and change the subject. Anyway, stay safe out there, fellow adventurers! The river is full of surprises. Some are geological. Some are hydrological. And some… I don’t think have names.
